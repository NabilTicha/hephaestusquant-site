Doctrine

Principles.

These govern how we conduct research, evaluate evidence, and make decisions. They are not aspirational. They are operational.

01

Falsification over confirmation

We design research to reject hypotheses, not to confirm them. A hypothesis that cannot be falsified has no place in our process. We actively seek disconfirming evidence before accepting any claim.

02

Evidence over narrative

A compelling story is not a substitute for statistical evidence. If the data does not support a thesis, the thesis is wrong — regardless of how intuitive or well-reasoned it appears.

03

Reproducibility over intuition

Any result that cannot be independently reproduced from the same inputs and methodology is not a result. Personal conviction is irrelevant without a reproducible process.

04

Risk before return

The first question is always about risk — where it comes from, how it compounds, and under what conditions it becomes non-linear. Return is a residual of managed risk, not a goal in itself.

05

Regime awareness over static optimization

Parameters calibrated to one regime will fail in another. Every model must account for structural change. Stationarity is a working assumption, not a fact.

06

Capital preservation bias

Asymmetric risk management. The cost of a missed opportunity is always lower than the cost of a realized loss. We err on the side of preservation.

07

Separation of research and deployment

A research claim is not a strategy. The decision to deploy capital requires an independent evaluation process with its own criteria, distinct from the research that generated the signal.
